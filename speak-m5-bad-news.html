Moment 5 of 8

Breaking Bad News

This is the moment most candidates rush — and rushing is exactly how you lose it. The marks here aren't for knowing the diagnosis; they're for how you hand it over: finding out what they already know, firing a warning shot, saying it plainly once, and then responding to the person in front of you instead of outrunning their reaction with a management plan.

Pattern 01

Start from what they already know

What many candidates say"I've got your results here. They show cancer."
becomes
What scores in the room"Before I go through the results — can I ask what your understanding is of why we did the tests?"

Finding out what they already know isn't a courtesy you can skip — it's marked, and it tells you whether they're braced for this or have no idea. It lets you pitch the news at exactly the right level rather than guessing.

Your turn

You're about to give a serious diagnosis. Open by checking their starting point.

"Before we talk about what the scan showed — what have you been told so far, and what have you been thinking yourself?"

Two doors in one line: what they've been told, and what they privately fear.

Pattern 02

Fire a warning shot

What many candidates say"So the biopsy's come back, and it shows cancer."
becomes
What scores in the room"I'm afraid I've got some difficult news about the results."  (then stop)

"I'm afraid…" is the classic warning shot — it gives the patient a second or two to brace before the news lands. It's one of the most recognised behaviours in a breaking-bad-news station. After it, you stop, and let them tell you they're ready.

Your turn

Give the warning shot for an unexpected serious result — then nothing else.

"I'm sorry to say the results weren't what we'd hoped for. … Is it okay if I talk you through them?"

Warning shot, pause, then a gentle check that they're ready to hear it. You don't barrel on.

Pattern 03

Say it plainly, once — then stop talking

What many candidates say"The histology demonstrates a malignant process consistent with adenocarcinoma."
becomes
What scores in the room"I'm sorry — the tests have shown that this is cancer."  (then say nothing)

Plain words, one clear sentence — then silence. Jargon like "malignant process" hides the news, and the patient has to actually understand they've been told. After you say it, stop: the silence isn't what's marked, but it's the only way you'll see and respond to their reaction, which is.

Your turn

Deliver the words "it's spread" without a single piece of jargon, then go quiet.

"I'm afraid the cancer has spread beyond where it started."  (silence)

Real words — "spread," not "metastatic." Said once. Then you wait, and you watch.

Pattern 04

Respond to the reaction — don't outrun it

What many candidates say"…so the next step is a referral to oncology for chemo, and we'll arrange a CT, and—"
becomes
What scores in the room"I can see this is a shock. Take whatever time you need — I'm right here."

Once the news is out, the marked behaviour is responding to their emotion — not delivering your plan. Acknowledge it, give space, and only move to "what happens next" when they're ready, which is usually when they ask. A management plan recited over someone's shock scores nothing.

Your turn

The patient goes silent and stares at the floor. Don't fill it with a plan.

"Take all the time you need. … Would it help if I explained what happens from here, or would you rather just sit with it for a moment?"

You hand them the choice of when to move on. Their pace, not your agenda.

Pattern 05

Be honest without taking away hope

What many candidates say"Don't worry, we'll get rid of it completely."  /  "I'm afraid there's nothing more we can do."
becomes
What scores in the room"I can't promise what's ahead, but you won't face it alone — there's a whole team around you, and we'll take this one step at a time."

Both false hope and blunt hopelessness lose marks and harm the patient. The calibrated middle is honest uncertainty plus committed support. "One step at a time" and "a team around you" carry real comfort without promising an outcome you can't guarantee.

Your turn

"Am I going to die?" Answer honestly, gently, without a false promise either way.

"That's such an important question, and I won't give you a glib answer. I can't tell you exactly what's ahead — but what I can promise is that we'll look after you every step, and we'll be honest with you the whole way."

Honesty about uncertainty, a promise about care. Never a false "no," never a brutal "yes."

Register notes — the breaking-bad-news voice

  • "I'm afraid…", "I'm sorry to say…" — the gentle warning-shot openers a UK doctor reaches for.
  • Real words, not soft ones. "Cancer," "spread," "we can't cure it" — not "malignancy," "metastasis," "palliative." Vagueness leaves people not realising they've been told.
  • After the news, your next line is about them, not your plan. Respond first; manage later.
  • "One step at a time", "a team around you", "I'm right here" — support that doesn't over-promise.

Where marks bleed when news is broken badly

  • No warning shot — the diagnosis lands like a slap.
  • Jargon the patient can't decode, so they don't actually understand what they've been told.
  • Talking through the silence and piling on information.
  • Jumping to the management plan before the news has landed.
  • False reassurance ("we'll cure it") or brutal finality ("nothing we can do").
